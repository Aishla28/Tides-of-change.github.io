Secondary interviews and insights

Internal migration, both within a state and across states in India, improves households' socioeconomic status, and benefits both the region that people migrate to and where they migrate from. Remittances can help reduce poverty in the migrants' places of origin.
But scheduled castes (SC)--castes considered lower in the social hierarchy--and scheduled tribes (ST)--indigenous tribal populations--benefited less from migration as social discrimination continued to impact them in the places they migrated to, research shows.



"This city was made of herds of castes. Even localities were identified by castes". Limbale is unable to secure a rental room unless he keeps his caste identity a secret, but that means living in fear of being exposed and facing violent consequences. Unable to transform his occupational mobility into social and geographic mobility, Limbale has no option but to live in a densely populated informal settlement with tenuous land rights and limited public services.



Ethnographic work on rural-urban migrants from the state of Jharkhand finds that the decision to move to the city often includes an expectation of liberation from rigid caste structures in villages. However, caste hierarchies and identities experienced by migrants do not disappear within cities, but are rather reorganized and reproduced as new collective identities emerge along reformulated caste lines, which are unique to the urban context. People belonging to scheduled casts or marginalized communities, do not only face problems due to their caste, but also due to their economic backgrounds. The above texts suggest that even if these people manage to break out of their brutal intersectionality by rising up the 'economic' ranks, they are still discriminated against due to their castes, that too by the ones belonging to upper castes. Hence, even though the country has progressed post globalization, it has not come out of its deep-rooted, discriminatory notions.
